ALOHA Lightning: Learning Fast and Precise Manipulation

Anonymous Authors,
Paper

Aloha Lightning learns precise, smooth, near-human speed policies. All videos are shown at 1x speed.

Abstract

We present ALOHA Lightning, a system for learning fast and precise robotic manipulation.

Learning from human demonstrations has enabled robots to acquire a wide range of manipulation skills, but learned policies typically execute far slower than ordinary humans. This speed gap is mainly due to lack of an interface for collecting demonstration data at high speed, and the difficulty in training policies that can robustly execute high-speed motions.

Our system uses kinesthetic teaching to intuitively collect near-human-speed demonstrations on a backdrivable bimanual platform, yielding natural and fast trajectories. We also present a learning pipeline that enables smooth high-speed execution through test-time action smoothing and aligns the visual data distribution between data collection and deployment with masking.

Given 50 demonstrations for each task, ALOHA Lightning autonomously completes tasks such as folding shorts, battery insertion, and bussing tables for over 80% success rates close to human speed.

Data Collection Demonstration

The ALOHA Lightning handles and camera mount are 3D printed. The lightweight setup is easy to move around and set up in different environments, and enables more sensory feedback during data collection. This enables collection of high speed and high quality demonstrations.

Puppeteering Setup

We compare ALOHA Lightning to a teleoperated puppeteering system where a human operator controls the robot using a pair of hand-held controllers. The puppeteering system is capable of precise motion, but requires extensive operator training and is very difficult to use at high speeds.

User Study Results

A user study where seven users, all with little to no prior teleoperation experience, were asked to perform the battery insertion task using both ALOHA Lightning and the puppeteering system. ALOHA Lightning enabled all users to collect demonstrations at high speeds, while the puppeteering system resulted in lower success rates and much slower execution times.

Puppeteering Speed-up Experiment

To achieve high speed inference, we attempt to speed up the puppeteering system in two ways: by doubling the control frequency from 50hz to 100hz, and by skipping every other action.

Comparison of puppeteering system performance at different speeds: 50Hz baseline, 100Hz frequency doubling, and action skipping approaches from left to right respectively. The double-frequency approach suffers from jerky motion causing imprecisions, and the skipping approach suffers from missing critical actions.

Alternative speed-up approaches on the puppeteering system result in significant performance degradation.

Test-Time Smoothing

To account for increased jerky behavior at chunk boundaries introduced by high speed inference, we introduce test-time smoothing.

Test-time smoothing is a light, easily adaptable approach where the user may specify the size of the smoothing window, as well as the degree of the smoothing via the sigmoid coefficient. This alleviates the discontinuities at chunk boundaries. For details, check out the paper.

Comparison of before (left) and after (right) test-time smoothing. Note the increased jerkiness of the unsmoothed version at chunk boundaries.

Test-time smoothing consistently improves success rates across all tasks, especially the more precise tasks such as battery insertion and folding shorts.
